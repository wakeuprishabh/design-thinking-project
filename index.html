Rethinking Social Media: A Design Thinking Approach

Tracing the roots of digital exhaustion, distraction, and the loss of safety.

Explore Project

Mission

Our mission is to explore the ethical and emotional impact of social media platforms and AI-driven systems on users. Through empathy-based research, we aim to design transparent, human-centered, and ethical digital experiences that promote trust, safety, and digital well-being.

Why Is It Important?

Social media plays a major role in the daily lives of young adults, shaping communication, entertainment, and access to information. However, excessive use, social comparison, misinformation, privacy risks, and digital dependency can negatively impact mental well-being,productivity, and online safety. This project is important because it aims to create healthier, safer, and more mindful digital experiences that better support users rather than exploit their attention.

Promotes Digital Well-Being

Encourages healthier social media habits by reducing stress, addictive usage, and emotional overload.

Improves Safety & Privacy

Addresses harassment, scams, misinformation, and weak privacy protections for safer online experiences.

Supports Productivity & Mindful Use

Helps users manage distractions, improve focus, and build healthier relationships with technology

Problem Statement Canvas

Using the integrated design thinking framework, we mapped our user, their context, pain points, root causes, and the scale of impact to arrive at a clear, actionable problem definition.

USER / PROTAGONIST

  • Young adults aged 18–25
  • Daily users of social media platforms
  • Use social media for communication, entertainment, information and social connection
  • Platforms used: Instagram, WhatsApp, YouTube, X, Reddit
  • Experiences stress, distraction, comparison pressure, privacy concerns, safety issues, and difficulty controlling usage

CONTEXT & SITUATION

  • Social media has become deeply integrated into daily life for young adults.
  • Users rely on it for staying connected with friends, consuming entertainment, professional networking, and keeping up with trends.
  • Frequent usage often leads to emotional overload, comparison pressure, productivity loss, privacy risks, and unhealthy dependency.

EVIDENCE BASE

  • Primary empathy interviews with users aged 18-25
  • Pew Research Center findings on Social Media and Mental Health
  • Direct user reports of excessive usage, doomscrolling, and anxiety
  • Identified patterns of productivity loss and comparison stress

PAIN POINTS & NEEDS

  • Difficulty controlling screen time due to addictive scrolling
  • Anxiety, stress, and low self-esteem caused by social comparison
  • Reduced productivity and focus
  • Privacy and safety concerns (harassment, scams, unsafe interactions)
  • Exposure to misinformation, toxic content, and unrealistic standards
  • Pressure to stay constantly available (FOMO)
  • Reduced attention span from short-form content
  • Need for better screen-time management & mindful usage tools
  • Need for stronger privacy, safety protections, and effective moderation
  • Need for healthier and less overwhelming social media experiences

ROOT CAUSES (5 WHYS)

  • Platforms are designed to keep users engaged for as long as possible rather than encouraging healthy usage.
  • Revenue models depend on capturing user attention, making excessive engagement a built-in incentive.
  • Features that encourage likes, visibility, and constant updates increase comparison, emotional dependence, and FOMO.
  • Safety and privacy systems are not strong enough to effectively prevent harmful interactions or misuse.
  • Fast-paced, short-form content encourages habitual consumption and makes sustained focus more difficult.

IMPACT & SCALE

Impact:

  • Increases stress, anxiety, and emotional overwhelm
  • Contributes to low self-esteem through constant comparison
  • Reduces productivity, focus, and academic/work performance
  • Encourages unhealthy digital dependency
  • Exposes users to harassment, privacy risks, and misinformation

Scale:

  • Affects millions of young social media users globally
  • Deeply integrated into daily communication
  • Impacts users across platforms (Instagram, TikTok, WhatsApp, etc.)
  • Increasing platform usage continues to expand the scale

Point of View Statement:

Young adult social media users (18–25) need a healthier and safer way to engage with social media because constant exposure to addictive content, social comparison, and digital distractions negatively impacts their mental well-being, productivity, and online safety

Research

Methodology & Scope

We conducted semi-structured empathy interviews with 8 participants aged 18–25, consisting of frequent daily users across platforms like Instagram, WhatsApp, YouTube, X, and Reddit.

Themes Explored:

Usage habits, emotional impact, comparison culture, privacy, safety, misinformation, productivity, and personal coping mechanisms.

Primary Findings

Results from our 8 user empathy interviews:

  • 100% (8/8) reported using social media daily.
  • 7/8 described excessive usage, doomscrolling, or difficulty controlling time spent.
  • 6/8 reported anxiety, comparison stress, or reduced self-esteem.
  • 6/8 reported intentionally taking breaks or deactivating apps for mental well-being.
  • 5/8 reported productivity loss, attention fragmentation, or academic distraction.
  • 4/8 experienced safety/privacy concerns (harassment, scam accounts, creepy interactions).

Secondary Research

Contextual findings from the Pew Research Center (2025):

  • 74% say social media helps them feel connected with friends.
  • 45% say they spend too much time on social media.
  • 45% say it negatively affects their sleep.
  • 40% say social media harms their productivity.
  • 39% say social media overwhelms them with drama.

⚡ 6-3-5 Brainwriting
→Behavioral Velocity AI: Flag accounts based on the inhuman speed of rapid-fire actions (likes, DMs) immediately upon creation.
→The 48-Hour Waiting Room: New accounts enter a mandatory 48-hour shadow period where their posts and comments are vetted by AI.
→Cross-Platform Hashing: Share anonymized hashes of known threat actors across networks for simultaneous internet-wide bans.
→Honeypot Accounts: Deploy invisible AI 'bot-trap' accounts; any new account that interacts with them is flagged as a scraper/spammer.
→Semantic Risk Scoring: Use NLP to analyze the phrasing of a user’s first 5 posts or DMs; high-risk scores trigger immediate manual review.
→Peer Sponsorship Activation: Accounts remain in 'read-only' mode until an existing, well-standing user clicks a link to 'sponsor' their entry.
→Predictive DM Intercept: AI scans outbound messages for predatory patterns and blocks delivery before the recipient ever sees it.
→Mandatory Boundary Onboarding: Users cannot access the feed until they explicitly complete a gamified privacy and trigger-warning setup.
→Default-Off Culture: All accounts default to maximum privacy (no stranger DMs, no public visibility) until the user actively toggles them on.
→Typing Nudges: If sentiment analysis detects highly aggressive typing, an interstitial pops up saying, 'This looks heated. Take a breath.'
→Community Pre-Juries: Empower highly trusted users to pre-screen content from accounts with a history of borderline violations.
→Dynamic Shielding: If an account suddenly receives a massive influx of mentions, replies auto-lock to 'Followers Only' to prevent dogpiling.
→Friction Gestures: Require a complex gesture (like a two-finger long press) to 'Like' a post, forcing a micro-moment of intentionality.
→Weighting Depth: Program the algorithm to weigh one thoughtful 50-word comment as 100x more valuable than a passive 'Like'.
→Chronological Sanctuaries: Allow users to pin a purely chronological, algorithm-free feed of mutual friends as their default home screen.
→Interaction Budgets: Give users a finite number of 'Likes' per day so they treat them as valuable currency rather than a reflexive tap.
→Categorized Reactions: Replace the generic Like button with specific tags: 'Helpful,' 'Changed My Mind,' or 'Empathize.'
→Sharing Reflection: When hitting share, prompt the user with: 'Why do you think your network needs to see this?' to reduce broadcasting.
→Ad-Free Learning Tiers: Users pay a small subscription fee for a feed optimized strictly for educational density, not retention.
→B2B Data Pivot: Shift revenue from consumer ad-clicks to providing enterprise sentiment and cultural trend analytics.
→Creator-Funded Networks: Users pay creators directly via micro-transactions; the platform takes a flat infrastructure cut.
→B-Corp Mandates: Restructure the company as a Public Benefit Corporation, legally tying executive duty to user well-being.
→Ethical Reach Pricing: Charge advertisers a premium to place ads in feeds that score high on user happiness and low on toxicity.
→Premium Creation Tools: Give consumption away for free, monetize via highly advanced, AI-driven professional editing tools for creators.
→Expert Verification Badges: Verify actual domain experts (doctors, climatologists) and algorithmically boost their replies.
→Semantic Fact-Checking API: Automatically cross-reference viral claims against databases and attach un-removable context labels.
→Downgrade Outrage: Train models to detect highly emotive, polarizing text and drastically reduce its organic reach.
→Penalty Multipliers: Accounts frequently debunked by community fact-checkers suffer compounding, invisible algorithm downgrades.
→Primary Source Boosts: Posts containing links to verified primary sources (.gov, .edu) receive an automatic visibility multiplier.
→The 'Citation Needed' Button: Allow users to vote for a claim to be reviewed; if threshold is met, visibility is paused until verified.
→Reality Prompts: A gentle pop-up appears before publishing: 'Does this post represent your actual day, or a highlight?'
→AI Edit Watermarks: Automatically detect heavily airbrushed images and permanently apply an 'Altered Image' badge.
→Throttle Glamour: If screen-time on highly edited influencer pages spikes, actively pivot the algorithm to show casual posts from IRL friends.
→Mandatory Dual-Camera: Create algorithmic incentives for 'BeReal' style posts that require showing the messy background behind the user.
→Ban Beauty Filters: Completely remove face-altering, pore-smoothing filters from the app's native camera tools.
→'Mundane Monday' Campaigns: Algorithmically boost posts on specific days where users are encouraged to share mundane or failing moments.

Solution: Prototype ReSocial

Core Concept: Intentional Social Media Redesign

ReSocial is a redesigned social media platform created to address the root causes identified in the analysis rather than simply treating the symptoms. It shifts the focus from addictive engagement and passive scrolling to intentional, user-controlled interaction that prioritises well-being, safety, and meaningful connection.

Finite Content Sessions

Instead of endless scrolling and infinite feeds, ReSocial provides users with limited content sessions that have a clear stopping point. This helps reduce overconsumption, doomscrolling, and the habit-forming behaviour caused by traditional platform design.

Intent-Based Platform Modes

Before entering, users choose their purpose to prevent content from being mixed into a single addictive feed:

  • Connect: Messaging & staying in touch
  • Learn: Verified educational content
  • Create: Content sharing & expression
  • Relax: Light entertainment

Reduced Social Comparison

Public likes, follower counts, and popularity-based metrics are removed to reduce validation-seeking behaviour, comparison anxiety, and fear of missing out. The platform focuses on meaningful interaction rather than visible popularity.

Transparent Recommendations

Instead of hidden engagement-driven algorithms, ReSocial provides transparent recommendation systems where users can understand why content appears and customise what they see, reducing manipulation and irrelevant content overload.

Safety & Privacy by Default

All accounts begin with maximum privacy settings enabled. Stranger messages, public visibility, and location sharing remain disabled unless actively enabled. AI-powered moderation proactively detects harassment and scam accounts.

Meaningful Engagement Model

Rather than rewarding passive scrolling, ReSocial encourages thoughtful conversations, genuine social interaction, and quality engagement. The design promotes healthier digital habits by prioritising intentional communication.

Reflections

REFLECTION 01

Users Don’t Hate Social Media, They Hate Losing Control

We assumed people wanted to quit social media, but most users actually enjoy connection, entertainment, and staying updated. The real frustration was feeling trapped in unhealthy habits they couldn’t regulate.

  • ✦ The problem is loss of control, not social media itself
  • ✦ Users want balance, not complete disconnection
  • ✦ Solutions should empower, not restrict
REFLECTION 02

Emotional Triggers Matter More Than Time Spent

Screen time alone didn’t explain the problem. A 30-minute session could leave someone motivated, while 10 minutes of comparison-heavy scrolling could create anxiety. Emotional impact proved more important than usage duration.

  • ✦ Time spent ≠ wellbeing impact
  • ✦ Content quality matters more than quantity
  • ✦ Emotional awareness should be part of the solution
REFLECTION 03

The Platforms Are Designed to Win Attention

Users blamed themselves for poor self-control, but our research showed many addictive behaviors are intentionally reinforced through infinite scroll, notifications, and short-form dopamine loops. The issue is partly systemic.

  • ✦ Overuse is influenced by platform design
  • ✦ Attention is actively engineered, not passively lost
  • ✦ Ethical design can counter manipulative patterns
REFLECTION 04

One Problem Creates Another

Social media issues weren’t isolated. Doomscrolling reduced sleep, poor sleep hurt focus, lower productivity increased stress, and stress pushed users back to social media. We discovered a self-reinforcing cycle.

  • ✦ Problems are interconnected, not independent
  • ✦ Breaking one loop can improve multiple outcomes
  • ✦ Systems thinking improved our solution design
REFLECTION 05

People Need Prevention, Not Just Recovery

Most digital wellbeing tools act after the damage is done—showing screen-time stats once users are already exhausted. Users needed early interventions before anxiety, distraction, or doomscrolling escalated.

  • ✦ Prevention is stronger than post-use reflection
  • ✦ Early nudges create healthier behavior
  • ✦ Real-time support feels more useful than reports
